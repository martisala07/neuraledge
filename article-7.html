Self-Driving Cars in 2025: Where Are We Now?

After a decade of promises and setbacks, autonomous vehicles are quietly becoming a real part of the transportation landscape — in some cities, at least. The full story is more nuanced than the headlines suggest.

By NeuralEdge Editorial February 19, 2025 11 min read
Waymo's fully autonomous robotaxis now operate commercially in multiple US cities with no human safety driver.

In 2015, Elon Musk predicted that Tesla would have fully autonomous cross-country vehicles within two years. In 2018, Waymo CEO John Krafcik told reporters that "the technology is ready." In 2020, multiple automakers announced that Level 5 autonomy — fully driverless under all conditions — was imminent. None of those predictions came true on schedule.

And yet, something significant has happened in the past few years. Autonomous vehicles are no longer purely a promise. They're operating commercially on public roads, carrying real passengers, without a human safety driver behind the wheel. The progress is real — but the full story requires understanding what different "levels" of autonomy actually mean, and how far we still have to go.

The SAE Autonomy Levels Explained

The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) defines six levels of driving automation, from 0 to 5:

  • Level 0 — No automation. The human does everything.
  • Level 1 — Driver assistance. Systems like adaptive cruise control or lane-keeping assistance handle one task at a time.
  • Level 2 — Partial automation. The system handles both steering and acceleration/braking simultaneously, but the driver must remain alert and ready to intervene at all times. Tesla Autopilot and GM Super Cruise operate at this level.
  • Level 3 — Conditional automation. The system can handle all driving in specific conditions; the driver can disengage attention but must be ready to take over when the system requests it.
  • Level 4 — High automation. The vehicle can handle all driving within a defined operational domain (such as a specific geographic area) without human intervention.
  • Level 5 — Full automation. The vehicle can drive anywhere, in any conditions, with no human required.

Most of the excitement and the controversy in autonomous vehicles centers on the jump from Level 2 to Levels 4 and 5. It's a much harder jump than it looks.

Where We Actually Are: The Waymo Story

Waymo, the autonomous vehicle company spun out of Google's self-driving project in 2016, is the clearest proof that Level 4 autonomy is real and commercially viable — within specific boundaries.

Waymo One, the company's commercial robotaxi service, operates in Phoenix, San Francisco, and Los Angeles. The vehicles — modified Jaguar I-PACE SUVs equipped with lidar, radar, and cameras — carry passengers with no human safety driver. As of early 2025, Waymo was completing over 150,000 paid trips per week across its operating markets.

The safety data is genuinely impressive. An independent analysis published in 2024 found Waymo's vehicles were involved in injury-causing crashes at a rate significantly lower than human drivers in comparable conditions. For proponents of autonomous vehicles, this is the crucial data point: automated systems, at their current level of capability, may already be safer than humans in the specific environments where they operate.

Tesla: A Different Philosophy

Tesla has taken a fundamentally different approach to autonomy than Waymo. Where Waymo relies on high-definition pre-mapped roads and a suite of sensors including lidar, Tesla's Full Self-Driving (FSD) system relies entirely on cameras and neural networks — an approach Tesla calls "vision-first." The philosophy is that humans drive with just their eyes and a brain; AI should be able to do the same.

Tesla's approach enables it to operate in any geography where its vehicles travel, without requiring expensive pre-mapping. But critics argue that cameras alone cannot provide the reliability needed for Level 4 autonomy, particularly in difficult conditions like heavy rain, fog, or construction zones where visual cues are ambiguous.

FSD is currently a Level 2 system — impressive in many situations, but requiring active driver supervision. Tesla's timeline for achieving unsupervised Level 4 capability has slipped repeatedly. The company continues to iterate rapidly, using data from millions of real-world miles driven by FSD-equipped vehicles to train its neural networks.

The Trucking Frontier

While robotaxis get most of the media attention, autonomous trucking has made significant commercial progress. Aurora Innovation launched commercial driverless truck operations on the Dallas-Houston corridor in April 2024 — becoming the first company to operate autonomous semi-trucks on public highways without a safety driver.

Long-haul trucking is in some ways a more tractable problem than urban robotaxi operation: highways are more predictable than city streets, weather conditions can be monitored in advance, and the economic case for automation is compelling given the chronic shortage of truck drivers.

"Autonomous vehicles are not arriving as a revolution. They're arriving as a gradual, geography-by-geography, use-case-by-use-case expansion of what's possible."

The Remaining Challenges

The gap between Level 4 in controlled environments and Level 5 everywhere is enormous, and reflects deep technical challenges that have proven surprisingly difficult to solve.

Edge cases and long-tail events — Autonomous systems handle common driving situations well. But driving involves an almost infinite variety of unusual situations: flooded roads, unusual road signage, improperly parked cars, children chasing balls, emergency vehicles approaching from unusual directions. Training a system to handle all of these reliably requires either enormous amounts of data or fundamentally new approaches to machine reasoning.

Sensor limitations — Cameras struggle in low light and poor weather. Lidar (laser-based ranging) is expensive and has limited range. Radar is robust in weather but low-resolution. No single sensor modality is sufficient; the fusion of multiple sensor types adds complexity and failure modes.

Regulation and liability — Regulatory frameworks for autonomous vehicles vary enormously by jurisdiction and are still evolving. Questions of liability — who is responsible when an autonomous vehicle is involved in a collision? — remain partially unresolved in most legal systems.

The Realistic Outlook

The honest assessment of self-driving cars in 2025 is: real, significant, commercially deployed progress in defined geographies and use cases — combined with a much longer timeline to truly universal autonomy than the industry once promised.

Waymo-style geofenced robotaxis will continue to expand to more cities. Autonomous trucking will grow on well-mapped interstate corridors. Semi-autonomous systems in consumer vehicles will become more capable and reliable. But the scenario of buying any car and telling it to drive you anywhere, in any weather, without any possibility of needing to intervene — that remains years or decades away, depending on who you ask.

In the meantime, the technology is saving real lives and moving real people — which is exactly the kind of incremental, evidence-based progress that tends to be most durable.
